The future of punishment and restoration in the U.S. criminal justice system

Restorative justice programs can be successful alternatives to imprisonment, but experts argue there is a long way to go before the practice becomes a large-scale solution.

ByPavi Rajesh, Elijiah Nicholson-Messmer, Kaitlyn Fiery, Shuang Jing, Benjamin Tan and Rui Sun

Thalia González wants to shut down the prison system. She's also a law professor. And like many restorative justice advocates, she's grappling with a tension inherent to every social movement: how do you work within the existing system while pushing for a future that looks very different.

“I'm an abolitionist,” said González who teaches at the University of California San Francisco, “but I also know that tomorrow a young person is going to stand up in court. If they could have a restorative option as opposed to not, I want that option to be available.”

González discovered restorative justice as a law student at Northwestern University when she worked as a circle practitioner, leading discussions between offenders and victims at a juvenile detention facility in Chicago. Since then, she’s become a leading expert in the field and has conducted extensive research on the opportunities and limitations of restorative justice, both domestically and abroad.

While advocates like González argue that restorative justice can’t fix all of the problems at the root of the criminal justice system, they believe it can chip away at the American prison-industrial complex which primarily has one broad tool to address any crime from non-violent drug offenses to homicide: incarceration.

Victims of crime aren’t satisfied with incarceration as a tool to address the harms done to them. Between 2006 and 2019, 52% of all violent crimes were left unreported to the police.“If you think about the way our criminal legal system currently works, there is little to no room within the structure of a court case for the victims,” said Susan Maze-Rothstein, the co-director of the Center for Restorative Justice at Suffolk University. “They are relegated to the role of witness. And so, arguably, what we are doing currently is more retraumatizing because people are left with their harm and their pain.”

“ If you think about the way our criminal legal system currently works, there is little to no room within the structure of a court case for the victims.”

Susan Maze-Rothstein, Co-director of the Center for Restorative Justice at Suffolk University

The growth of restorative justice

The need for alternative responses to crime, along with promising studies on the effectiveness of restorative justice in lowering recidivism rates, has led to increased federal funding and a growth of restorative justice programs across the country in settings such as schools and prisons.

Last year, the U.S. Department of Justice announced it would award almost $57 million to support criminal justice reform programs and advance racial equity within the criminal justice system. From that budget, $8 million specifically went toward promoting restorative justice programs among other similar causes. In addition, experts in the growing field are meeting to share ideas and insights from their work.

Still, advocates point to the lack of a national framework on what qualifies as restorative justice or how it should be used.

“We don't even have a comprehensive map of how many prosecutors and officers are using restorative justice,” said González. “It's hard to know what you know when you don't know what the hell's going on.”

A shift toward accountability and healing

“We are in a place right now where our system is very punitive and has very high sentences,” said Adriaan Laani, the Touroff-Glueck Professor of Law at Harvard University. “That has created an expectation among people that the only way to take harm seriously is to have a very high sentence …. You have to get over this assumption that people have that a restorative process is somehow not taking it seriously. Restorative justice can really get at true accountability in a way that the regular criminal process of incarceration does not.”

“ We are in a place right now where our system is very punitive and has very high sentences, and that has created a kind of expectation among people that the only way to take harm seriously is to have a very high sentence.”

Adriaan Laani, the Touroff-Glueck Professor of Law at Harvard University

Circles of Support and Accountability (CoSA) programs have been used to reintegrate high-risk offenders in Vermont for the past 20 years. Studies show offenders who participated in CoSA programs were less likely to commit new crimes.

Despite successes, growing pains persist

“There’s a huge issue of equal access to restorative justice and equity in particular,” said Laani. “One [question] is whether or not all communities should have access to restorative justice. Ideally, you don’t want a kind of discretionary system where it’s just up in the air, but you want to have a very clear criteria for when people are given the option of taking a restorative process.”

At the moment, these programs are rare and only accessible to a fraction of offenders. Experts like Lindsey Pointer, an assistant professor at Vermont Law and Graduate School and the principal investigator of the National Center on Restorative Justice worry that that giving judges and police officers final say over who qualifies to participate in restorative justice creates room for bias based on offender’s race, gender, type of crime and criminal history.

“We have something that's shown a lot of promise in terms of reforming the system, but we run a significant risk if we have these referral structures that could be replicating the same bias that we see elsewhere. We could end up actually exacerbating the differences in outcomes for people if we're only having young white people from affluent backgrounds being referred, for example.”

“In the U.S., our attachment to a racial capitalist society has been adherent to incarcerating Black and Brown folks for centuries,” said González. “This isn't new business. Restorative justice isn't going to solve that.”

Beyond the criminal justice system

Howard Zehr, a pioneering advocate of restorative justice, said when he began his work in the late 1970s, restorative justice addressed problems in the criminal legal system. As the movement has grown, however, it has been tried in other settings—from the Smithsonian Museum of American History to South Africa’s Truth and Reconciliation Commission and even American schools.

Many advocates see more promise in implementing restorative justice efforts upstream of incarceration, before a serious crime has been committed.

“It is in that building of community, maintaining and sustaining community with these practices that you’re actually creating justice,” said Maze-Rothstein.

Restorative justice was not originally linked to crime. Modern-day restorative justice practices have their roots in indigenous communities, including Māori and Navajo traditions. Through a process which some tribes called “peacemaking,” Native American communities often worked through disputes by focusing on repairing relationships and finding solutions instead of placing blame and dishing punishment. Consequently, many experts believe these values are so strong that they support all facets of community, not just when a crime has been committed.

As Maze-Rothstein said, “There is no human activity that could not benefit from these practices.”

Barriers remain, but advocates are optimistic

But getting widespread support for restorative justice is still elusive. Underlying its difficult adaptation into the broader criminal justice system is what Pointer calls a “public imagination problem.”

“That is the hurdle [that] we need to get over in terms of just expanding what that word justice can mean for people,” said Pointer. “So, can justice mean understanding impact and repairing harm? Can justice mean transforming the structural and equity characteristics that led to the need that fueled the behavior that caused harm in the first place? Once we're past that hurdle, once that public imagination is expanded, then I think we would be able to do something like what New Zealand, for example, has done, where restorative justice is a default option.”

New Zealand has one of the most comprehensive national restorative justice systems in the world, according to Zehr. But, even the current framework there does not fully incorporate itRJ into the justice system. Outlining what a national restorative justice system could look like is a barrier for the movement, Zehr said.

When Pointer introduces people to restorative justice, they can be quick to dismiss it as a utopian idea. “I get that,” Pointer said. “But I just try to help people see [the] common human need, [the] common human experience in the wake of crime. And then it's less utopian, it's less idealistic about people and it's more just actually responsive to the needs [of the harmed party].”

Expanding that public imagination is a slow process, advocates say. But, the growth of restorative justice in recent years demonstrates it as a possibility, one advocates are pushing for everyday.
